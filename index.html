Frequently asked questions

Short answers to help users understand how the draft generator fits into the request process.

What is a public records request?

It's a formal request to a government agency asking them to provide copies of or access to documents they hold. Every state has a law guaranteeing the public's right to inspect government records. These laws go by different names - FOIA, Open Records Act, Right to Know Law, Sunshine Law - but they all serve the same purpose: government transparency.

Do I need a lawyer to file a request?

No. Anyone can file a public records request. You don't need a lawyer, special training, or any particular reason. A well-written letter citing the correct law is all you need, which is exactly what this tool helps you create.

Does it cost anything to file a request?

Filing the request itself is free. However, most agencies can charge fees for making copies, and some charge for the staff time to search for records. Fees vary by state and agency. Some states cap per-page costs, for example Illinois gives you the first 50 pages free. This tool includes relevant fee information for each state in the generated letter.

How long will it take to get my records?

It depends on the state and the complexity of your request. Some states have strict deadlines, such as Illinois requiring a response within 5 business days, while others only require a response within a "reasonable" time. The letter this tool generates includes the applicable deadline for your state so the agency knows you're aware of it.

Can my request be denied?

Yes, but only for specific legal reasons. Every state has exemptions - categories of records that don't have to be disclosed, like ongoing criminal investigations, trade secrets, or certain personnel records. If your request is denied, the agency must tell you why and cite the specific exemption. Many states also have an appeals process.

Can I request records from any state, or only the one I live in?

Most states allow anyone to make a request regardless of where they live. However, a handful of states, including Alabama, Virginia, and others, require the requester to be a resident of that state or have a specific connection. This tool alerts you when a state has a residency requirement.

What kinds of records can I request?

Generally, anything created or maintained by a government agency as part of its official business - emails, contracts, reports, budgets, meeting minutes, correspondence, inspection records, permits, policies, and more. The records can be in any format: paper, electronic, audio, video.

What if I don't know which agency has the records I need?

Start with the agency most likely to be involved. For example, if you want building permits, try the city or county building department. If you want police records, contact the police department directly. Your state's general government directory or 211 helpline can also help you identify the right agency.

Can I request records from private companies?

No. Public records laws only apply to government agencies. For corporate records, you may need to explore other options like SEC filings, court records, or direct requests to the company.

Is this tool a substitute for legal advice?

No. This tool generates request letters based on publicly available state law information. It is not legal advice. If your request involves sensitive legal matters or if your request is denied and you want to pursue legal action, consult an attorney familiar with your state's public records law.
